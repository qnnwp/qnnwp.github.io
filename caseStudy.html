Overview

T-Mobile Tuesdays is a customer appreciation app that gives T-Mobile subscribers free gifts every tuesday. It was released with usability problems and has a current rating of 3 on Itunes. My team was tasked with adding features and redesigning aspects of the app to solve users' goals.


Problem

T-Mobile Tuesdays was created to increase brand loyalty and resubscribe rate. Initial usability problems caused it to be poorly rated, but current users find the app to be simple and easy to use. The current problem with the app is the value it’s offers are providing. Too many of the offers are for products that the users aren’t interested in or can’t use. The app is also only ever used on tuesdays, and many users forget about it’s existence, which undermines T-Mobile’s goals for the app.


Research


Domain Research

To start, we did domain research. We learned that T-Mobile Tuesdays was created as part of the Uncarrier program to increase brand loyalty and resubscribe rate. This gave us a greater understanding of the business goals for the app.

Application Audit

Next, we did an audit of the current app. We went through the app multiple times, and documented the flow of the app through pictures to make sure each member of the team had a clear understanding of what was currently available and possible with the app.

Comparative Analysis

To finish our background research, we did a competitive/comparative analysis on the other carriers and a few other reward programs. This gave us a better understanding of the competition for T-Mobile and our app.

Interviews

After we had enough background research done on the app, we created an interview script, and then did 9 user interview’s. From the 9 interview’s, we created a document with snippets of the most interesting comments so that each team member could get a better sense of all of the interview answers.


Analysis


Affinity diagrams

From our user interview, we immediately created Affinity Diagrams. From these, we learned that our users liked certain consistent giveaways, wanted variety, wanted personalized giveaways, and enjoyed the simple UI.

Personas

From our affinity diagrams and user research, we settled on two persona’s: one for our gen-x users, and our main persona for our millennials. Each represented the goals, behaviors, and pain points of their respective users.


Design & Testing


Design Studio

To start our design process, we did a design studio to generate ideas that solved the pain points and goals of our personas. We then narrowed the ideas we created to our 15 favorites.

From there, we did a second iteration of design studio to flesh out those fifteen ideas. We each dug into our favorite ideas and tried to flesh them out.

Initial Design

We then ended up deciding on three new ideas for the app:

  1. We decided to add a personalization section that would allow users to choose categories of prizes that they wanted to receive.
  2. We next decided to add a weekly games section that would let users play games on off days for extra entries into the weekly contest
  3. We finally decided to add the ability to share prizes with friends instead of redeeming them.

Personalization

One of the biggest pain points with the app was not enough offers that actually applied to our customers. Another was variety. Personalization can help with both of these problems. Personalization helps the user by giving them more offers that they actually want and also helps T-Mobile sell advertisers on partnering with them by giving them customer metrics. This should also help with the variety of offers that are offered. We initially decided to put a new section in the menu titled personalize to house this addition.

Sharing Prizes

We added the ability to share prizes. This allows our users to feel like they are putting more of the prizes they receive to use by sharing prizes they don’t want. It also would help spread the app to more users when prizes are shared with T-mobile users who don’t have the app. We decided to place this feature next to the redeem button because for business reasons, it made sense to have each prize to be used only once.

Games

We added Games so that the users interacted with the app more, and got more free things, the main reason they come back to the app. Our research showed that users primarily only open the app on tuesday, and interact with the app for a very short amount of time. We think that by adding games, users will respond more positively to the app. This also allows for more promotion of the featured brand throughout the week, and opens the possibility for game sponsorship partners, or a new partner who offered a reward through the games. This feature was added as an extra item in the menu.

Paper Prototype

We next created a paper prototype so that we could test and iterate.

Testing Overview

For usability testing, we did 3 rounds of four gorilla tests. We asked our user testers to complete tasks associated with our user flows, and then asked them for their general thoughts on how usable the app was, why they had any issues (if any) using the app, and what their overall thoughts were.

Usability Test: Round 1

Initially, we decided to have personalize be it’s own section in the menu. Throughout our first round of testing, every user looked in the settings when prompted to personalize their prizes. We thus moved the personalization section from the main menu to the settings. This also caused us to redesign the look of the menu as there was now a visual imbalance.

Usability Test: Round 2

The second round of user testing let us know that flow of playing a game from the normal app was confusing. After playing the game and winning one of the level of prizes, our users were hesitant to hit the button “go to my stuff”. After seeing a prize, they thought they might lose it by clicking on the button. We decided to change the button to read “add to my stuff”, which our user testers thought made much more sense.

Axure Mockup

We finally created a high fidelity mockup, and then ran further tests on it.

View Axure Mockup

Usability Test: Round 3

In the third round of user testing, we found that our users were confused by our personalization page. We decided to add a more accurate heading to give the users a better sense of what they were doing on that page. We also added a save button because the users were worried that hitting the X would cancel their changes.


Conclusions


Collaboration

As a team, we worked really well together. We split the work up well, while making sure to always be on the same page about where each of us were. We were all very good at being respectful about each other's opinions while also quickly coming to group decisions. Whenever we disagreed, we would quickly come to a consensus. We were all good about not being married to our own designs and our own opinions.

What I learned

  1. This was my first axure project. I learned that I need to plan my masters out before I start working. By not thinking about what items needed to be masters, I ended up having problems resizing the personalize page after we decided to add the save button and top message.
  2. I learned that I need to become better at expressing my design beliefs. I didn’t think that our addition of games solved any of our user’s needs. None of our users expressed that they were anything but neutral towards the current games, and I believe that the reward (extra raffle ticket) would not be enough to make playing the games every week not feel like a chore. I don’t think I expressed my opinion in the most clear and concise way, and it’s something I want to work on in the future.
  3. I learned a lot about how to express my needs as an introvert during this project. One of the group members is very extroverted, and I tried to always make it clear when I needed some time to think on my own, while always making sure to check in on regular intervals to share my work and get feedback about it.
