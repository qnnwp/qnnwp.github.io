My Story

Growing up

Growing up, I was always bored in school. Math and Science came easily, while English and History barely held any of my attention. I wasn’t the best at drawing, so I assumed I wasn’t creative. I coasted through high school on smarts and intuition., getting good but not great grades. I always got all of my work done, but I never felt like I fully applied myself.

Early College

I ended up going to college at the University of Washington, and entered having no idea what I wanted to do with my life or what to study. My first quarter I randomly took an introductory Computer Science course. It was the first time I had truly enjoyed a class, and I decided to pursue it as my major. I threw myself into the relevant classes, got a web development job, and felt purpose for the first time in my life. Receiving the acceptance letter was the proudest moment of my life! I couldn’t stop smiling.

Late College

Unfortunately, as I started taking more CS classes, I got less and less excited by the field. By the time I was graduating, I realized that I had only truly enjoyed two of the 300+ level classes: Statistics, and Algorithms. Graduation felt like it should have been a crowning achievement for my life: I had graduated from the most competitive major at my school, gotten pretty good grades, but instead, it felt like a trap.

Graduation

I felt lost. As my friends were applying and accepting jobs, I withdrew into a shell and barely applied to any. The thought of having to work a coding job 8 hours a day felt like a prison sentence. My mom would ask me to sit down and apply to jobs with her, and I would be frightened all weekend. Eventually, my mom and I had a conversation about why my job search was a non-starter, and we realized that a change was needed.

Carreer Counseling

I started seeing a career counselor. We talked about my many hobbies (Ultimate Frisbee, Fantasy/Sci fi books, Board Games, Sports), my personality type (INFP), what I enjoyed about coding (Algorithms, problem solving), and what I didn’t (bug fixing, coding). We came to the conclusion that I needed a career that involved a lot of creative problem solving. Through research and discussion, we narrowed it down to either industrial design or UX design.

Career Change

I realized that the most fulfilling parts of my web development job had been when I designed for the user’s experience. My boss and I would always ask each other, “What would be best for the user.”. I also figured that the transition to become a UX designer would be easier/cheaper, so it made the most sense to make a career change to become a UX designer.

Conclusion

I consider this career change to be the best “worst decision” of my life. Monetarily, it was a terrible choice. On the other hand, it was great for my personal happiness. I’ve been working harder on my UX projects than I ever did on the coding projects I had back in school, and most importantly, I’m excited for the future.

Work Environment

I want a work environment that promotes collaboration and individual work. As an introvert, I need my time alone to think and create, but the collaborative aspects of design are often when I have the most fun. I want to work on a variety of projects that challenge me to think in different ways, and solve many unique problems. I want hard deadlines that push my limits. And most of all, I need a positive, fun, and creative team that respects me and pushes me towards mastery of my trade.

K. Quinn Walker-Phelan

Seattle, Wa
